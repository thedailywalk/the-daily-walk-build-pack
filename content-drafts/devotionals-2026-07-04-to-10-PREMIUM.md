weekFocus: Following the God who leads — even through the wilderness.
dayLabel: Saturday · July 4
editorNote: Welcome to the deeper walk this week. We're moving through the wilderness of Numbers alongside Mark's Jesus — two pictures of one truth: the God who leads you can be trusted with your heart. Take these ten minutes slow.
devHeading: The border, the heart, and the voice you obey
devRef: Numbers 14–16 · Mark 7
devIntro: Israel reaches the edge of everything God promised — and freezes. Ten scouts describe giants; two describe God. The camp chooses the giants. Meanwhile Jesus, in Mark 7, quietly relocates the whole battle from the outside of a person to the inside.
devVerseText: "Nothing outside a person can defile them by going into them. Rather, it is what comes out of a person that defiles them."
devVerseRef: Mark 7:15
devBody: The real failure at the border isn't out there — it's the fear inside that believes the report and refuses the promise. In Mark, the religious leaders fixate on external contamination, and Jesus tells them they've got the geography of the human problem backwards. What defiles you was never mainly outside you; it rises up from within — the fear, pride, and unbelief that turned a two-week trip into a forty-year detour. And that's actually good news: because the real battle is the heart, it's a battle God can win in you no matter what's happening around you. The giants may not shrink today, but the voice you obey — fear's or God's — is decided on the inside, and grace meets you right there.
devKeyWord: Defile — to stain or make unclean. Jesus moves it from a hands-and-food problem to a heart problem: what marks you is what comes out of you, not what happens to you.
deeperWalk: For a first-century Jew, "clean" and "unclean" governed everything — what you ate, who you touched, whether you could approach God. So when Jesus calmly declares that a heart, not a hand, is what separates a person from God, it lands like a bomb. He isn't abolishing holiness; He's relocating it. Real defilement was never crumbs on unwashed fingers — it's what we incubate inside. Numbers shows the same truth from the opposite direction: a whole generation is kept out of the land not by Canaan's armies but by their own fearful hearts. The enemy outside couldn't stop them; the unbelief inside did. Your biggest battle today isn't your circumstances — it's the heart-level fear you keep agreeing with.
bibleThread: Israel's unclean hearts keep them out of the land; Mark 7 diagnoses the same disease in all of us — "out of the heart come evil thoughts." No amount of hand-washing fixes it. That's the ache the whole Bible is building toward: we need a new heart we cannot scrub ourselves into. On the cross, Jesus is treated as unclean — cursed, cut off — so that everyone who trusts Him is washed from the inside out. The border Israel couldn't cross, Jesus opens: a clean heart, given as a gift.
heartCheck: What "report" am I rehearsing right now — the fearful one about my life, or the faithful one about my God? | Where am I guarding the outside (appearance, performance) while ignoring what's growing inside? | Is there a fear I've been agreeing with that God never spoke over me?
journalPrompt: God, search my heart the way You searched Israel's. Where has fear quietly moved in — and what do You actually say over the very thing I'm afraid of? Write down what you hear.
wellnessPractice: When your body braces at the scary report today, pause. Take three slow breaths. On each exhale pray, "The giants are real, but You are bigger." Let your shoulders drop on the last one.
devPrayer: Father, You see the fear I keep believing over Your promise. Do Your quiet work on the inside of me — clean the heart, steady the nerve, and let Your voice be the one I obey today. I don't want to wander the long way around what You're holding out to me. Amen.
walkItOut: Catch one fearful report today and answer it out loud with a promise. Before the thing you're dreading, name the fear once — then name one true thing God has said — and move on the promise, not the panic.
saveLine: What defiles you was never mostly outside you — and grace meets you right in the heart.
tomorrowThread: Tomorrow: Jesus asks the question the whole story turns on — "Who do you say I am?" — then tells us what saying it will cost.
studyHeading: Weekend Study — When the ground says no and God says go
studyRef: Numbers 13–14
studyBody: Take the long view this weekend on the spy story — it's one of the Bible's clearest lessons on how fear and faith work. Twelve men see the identical land. All twelve agree it's good; they carry back fruit so heavy it takes two of them. The facts aren't in dispute — the interpretation is. Ten say, "We can't," and call themselves grasshoppers. Two — Caleb and Joshua — say, "We can, because the Lord is with us." Faith here isn't denial. Caleb doesn't pretend the cities are small; faith simply refuses to leave God out of the math. And the tragedy is that the fearful report wins the vote, and a whole generation inherits the exact future they feared. What we rehearse, we often walk into. The border moments of your life — the new job, the hard conversation, the obedience you've been circling — will always come with a giant-sized report. The question is never whether the giants are real. It's whether you'll factor in the God who is bigger.
studyKeyWord: Grasshoppers — how the fearful scouts saw themselves ("we seemed like grasshoppers"). Fear shrinks your view of yourself because it first shrinks your view of God.
studyVerse: "We should go up and take possession of the land, for we can certainly do it." — Numbers 13:30 (Caleb)
studyPause: Where has a fearful report been quietly outvoting the faithful one in you?
studyQuestion: What "border" is God asking you to step across this season — and what true thing about Him do you need to say louder than the fear?

dayLabel: Sunday · July 5
editorNote: Sunday is a good day to be asked a hard, simple question. Jesus asks it in today's reading — sit with it before the week starts.
devHeading: The question that costs you something
devRef: Numbers 17–20 · Mark 8
devIntro: God ends a leadership dispute by making a dead almond branch bud overnight — life from a dead stick. The wilderness grinds on: no water, Moses striking the rock in anger, Miriam and Aaron buried. And in Mark 8, right after feeding thousands, Jesus asks His friends who they say He is — then tells them what saying it will cost.
devVerseText: "Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me. For whoever wants to save their life will lose it, but whoever loses their life for me and for the gospel will save it."
devVerseRef: Mark 8:34–35
devBody: Peter gets the answer gloriously right — "You are the Messiah" — and then, in almost the same breath, gets the meaning wrong, recoiling when Jesus mentions a cross. So Jesus widens it to everyone: following Me isn't admiring Me from a comfortable distance; it's losing the life you were clutching so you can actually find one. That sounds grim until you notice what it rescues you from. The life we grip — our comfort, control, reputation — is exactly the life that keeps slipping through our fingers no matter how tight we hold. Jesus says: stop trying to save that. Hand it over, and I'll give you back something that can't be lost.
devKeyWord: Deny yourself — not self-hatred, but taking your hands off the throne. It's trading your grip on your own life for God's grip on it.
deeperWalk: Notice Mark's placement. The instant Peter confesses Jesus as Messiah, Jesus starts teaching about suffering and a cross — and Peter rebukes him. Peter wanted a Messiah who wins the way the world wins: power, thrones, no pain. Jesus insists the throne runs through a cross. That collision is the whole struggle of discipleship: we say yes to Jesus, then flinch at His methods. And the budding staff back in Numbers whispers the same gospel logic — God brings life out of a dead branch, not out of the ones straining hardest to look alive. Resurrection follows surrender, not self-preservation.
bibleThread: "Take up your cross" only means something because Jesus takes up His first. He is the true Israel who doesn't grumble in the wilderness, the leader whose authority buds like Aaron's staff — life out of death. Every call to lose your life to find it is grounded in the One who lost His to find you. You're not asked to die to earn God's love; you're asked to follow a Savior who already died to secure it, and to discover, on the far side of surrender, a life you could never have grasped for.
heartCheck: What version of my life am I gripping so tightly it can't become the life Jesus is offering? | Where do I want Jesus, but not His methods? | What would "losing my life for His sake" actually look like this week?
journalPrompt: Jesus, You ask for the very thing I'm most afraid to release. Name it with me here — and show me what I'd actually gain if I let You have it.
wellnessPractice: Sit still for one minute. As you breathe out, physically unclench your hands, palms up, and pray, "I'm taking my hands off. Yours are steadier than mine." Stay there a few breaths.
devPrayer: Jesus, I want to say "You are the Christ" and mean it all the way down — into the parts of my life I've been guarding. Teach me the strange math of Your kingdom: that what I lose for You, I actually keep. Loosen my grip today. Amen.
walkItOut: Name one thing you've been clutching — a comfort, a plan, a need to be right — and loosen your grip on it in one concrete way today. Let someone else be right; give away time you'd marked as yours. Notice you didn't lose your life. You found a little more of it.
saveLine: The life you're afraid to lose is the one He's asking to redeem.

weekFocus: Following the God who leads — even through the wilderness.
dayLabel: Monday · July 6
editorNote: Most Mondays don't need more willpower — they need somewhere to look. Today's reading gives you a direction.
devHeading: Everything is possible — for the One who believes
devRef: Numbers 21–23 · Mark 9
devIntro: In the desert, people are dying of snakebite, and God's cure is almost offensively simple: a bronze snake on a pole — look, and live. In Mark 9, Jesus blazes with glory on a mountain, then walks straight down into a father's impossible situation and an honest, half-built prayer.
devVerseText: "'If you can'?" said Jesus. "Everything is possible for one who believes." Immediately the boy's father exclaimed, "I do believe; help me overcome my unbelief!"
devVerseRef: Mark 9:23–24
devBody: Don't miss how gentle Jesus is with a shaky faith. The father hedges — "if you can do anything" — and Jesus turns the "if" back on him: the question was never My ability, it's whether you'll trust it. Then comes the most relatable prayer in Scripture: "I do believe; help my unbelief." He holds real faith and real doubt in the same sentence, and Jesus doesn't wait for him to resolve the tension before He acts. The bronze snake preaches the same sermon: the dying didn't have to manufacture strong feelings — they just had to look, in trust, at what God provided. So the pressure's off your faith being impressive. It only has to be pointed the right direction.
devKeyWord: Unbelief — not the absence of faith but the doubt tangled up with it. Jesus welcomes the honest "help my unbelief" over pretend certainty every time.
deeperWalk: The bronze serpent is one of the strangest cures in Scripture, and it's deliberately counterintuitive. God doesn't remove the snakes; He doesn't ask the people to fight or to earn healing. He asks them to look — to turn their eyes toward the very thing that looks like their curse, now lifted up, and trust. It's an early lesson that salvation is received, not achieved. Centuries later, Numbers 21 becomes the picture Jesus reaches for to explain His own cross (John 3:14). The healing was never in the bronze; it was in the God who told them to look. And the healing today is never in the strength of your feelings; it's in the One you turn your eyes toward.
bibleThread: "Just as Moses lifted up the snake in the wilderness, so the Son of Man must be lifted up, that everyone who believes may have eternal life in him." Jesus said that Himself. The pole in the desert was a rehearsal for the cross. The venom of sin runs through all of us, and the cure is the same as it was for Israel — not striving, not fixing, but looking in trust to the One lifted up for you. Monday's whole assignment is a lifted gaze: away from the bite, toward the Rescuer.
heartCheck: Where am I bringing Jesus a faith that's real but shaking? | Am I waiting to feel certain before I trust — instead of trusting with the faith I have? | What am I still trying to fix by fighting, when God is saying "look"?
journalPrompt: Jesus, here is exactly where my faith is strong and where it's shaky — I'll name both. Meet me in the middle of my honest, mixed-up trust and be strong where I'm weak.
wellnessPractice: Name the one worry you keep white-knuckling. Take three slow breaths, and on each exhale lift your eyes (literally look up) and pray, "I believe; help my unbelief." Leave it lifted.
devPrayer: Jesus, I believe; help my unbelief. Thank You that You don't require impressive faith before You come close — only a faith willing to look Your way. Be strong in the place I'm weak today. I'm looking up. Amen.
walkItOut: Take the one shaky area of your life and, out loud, pray the father's exact words over it: "I believe; help my unbelief." Then leave it lifted up in His hands instead of forcing a feeling — and go about your day.
saveLine: Healing came by looking, not by fighting. Keep your eyes up.
tomorrowThread: Tomorrow: a king hires a prophet to curse God's people — and blessing keeps falling out of his mouth instead. You can't be cursed out of what God has blessed.

weekFocus: Following the God who leads — even through the wilderness.
dayLabel: Tuesday · July 7
editorNote: There's a quiet comfort in today's reading for anyone who feels like something's working against them. Let it settle in.
devHeading: The blessing you can't be argued out of
devRef: Numbers 24–26
devIntro: A frightened king hires a prophet to curse Israel from a hilltop. Three times Balaam opens his mouth to curse; three times, blessing pours out instead. He physically cannot do the job — because a curse has no authority over what God has chosen to bless.
devVerseText: "May those who bless you be blessed and those who curse you be cursed!"
devVerseRef: Numbers 24:9
devBody: Sit in the strangeness of this. There's an entire operation running against God's people up on that ridge — money changing hands, a professional curse-maker, three attempts to speak ruin over them. And Israel, down in the valley, has no idea. They're just living an ordinary day while, unseen, God bends every intended curse into a blessing before it can land. Not every battle over your life happens where you can see it, and not every rescue comes with a headline. Some of the most important things God does for you, you'll never witness. Your assignment isn't to out-argue every threat — it's to stay planted under the blessing of the God who has the last word.
devKeyWord: Oracle — a message spoken for God. The irony of Numbers 24 is that even a prophet paid to harm God's people can only say what God gives him to say.
deeperWalk: Balaam is one of Scripture's strangest figures because he's genuinely spiritual and genuinely for sale. He hears from God, and he still tries three times to cash a check to curse God's people. That's the warning tucked inside the comfort: spiritual language is not the same as a surrendered heart. And notice how the danger finally gets in. What the hired curse could not do from the outside (chapter 24), quiet compromise nearly does from the inside (chapter 25) — Israel is lured into sin at Peor and thousands fall. The lesson stacks: no external curse can cancel God's blessing, but an unguarded heart can forfeit the enjoyment of it. Stand under the blessing — and still guard the gate.
bibleThread: Out of a hired pagan prophet's mouth comes one of the Old Testament's clearest glimpses of the coming King: "A star will come out of Jacob; a scepter will rise out of Israel" (24:17). Even a man paid to curse God's people ends up announcing their Messiah. That's how unstoppable the blessing is — it was always moving toward Jesus. The enemy cannot curse what God has blessed because the blessing has a name, and He is coming. Centuries later, another star will rise over Bethlehem and wise men will follow it to the King Balaam only glimpsed.
heartCheck: What have I been treating like a curse hanging over me? | Am I bracing against threats I can't see instead of resting under a blessing I can trust? | Where might quiet compromise be doing what no enemy could force on me?
journalPrompt: Father, name the thing I've been living braced against — and remind me what You've actually spoken over my life. Where do I need to guard my heart, and where do I need to simply rest?
wellnessPractice: Notice where your body is bracing — jaw, shoulders, stomach. Take three slow breaths, and on each exhale pray, "I don't have to defend what You've already blessed." Let the tension go.
devPrayer: Father, thank You that no plan against me gets the final say — You do. Where I've been bracing and anxious, remind me I stand under Your blessing, not someone else's curse. And guard my heart from the compromise no enemy could force on me. Amen.
walkItOut: Name one thing you've been managing with worry, and hand it up instead: "God, You turn curses into blessings; do that here, even if I never see how." Then go live your day in the valley, unafraid.
saveLine: You cannot be cursed out of what God has blessed you into.

dayLabel: Wednesday · July 8
editorNote: Midweek is when the pull to climb, achieve, and accumulate is strongest. Today Jesus turns the whole ladder upside down.
devHeading: Greatness, flipped upside down
devRef: Numbers 27–30 · Mark 10
devIntro: In Numbers, five sisters ask for an inheritance the law never made room for, and God says yes — the last get moved to the front. In Mark 10, a rich man walks away sad, the disciples argue about who's greatest, and Jesus quietly redefines the whole game.
devVerseText: "For even the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many."
devVerseRef: Mark 10:45
devBody: Mark 10 is full of people reaching up — the rich man reaching for eternal life without letting go of his wealth, James and John reaching for the best seats. And into all that reaching, Jesus drops a sentence that turns the ladder into a basin and a towel: the greatest among you will be the servant of all, because that's exactly how the King came. He didn't arrive to be waited on. He came to wash feet and lay down His life as a ransom. The world says you rise by being served; Jesus says you rise by descending — by spending yourself on people who can't repay you. And He's already at the bottom, towel in hand, having paid the ransom Himself.
devKeyWord: Ransom — the price paid to set a captive free. Jesus doesn't just teach servanthood; He funds your freedom with His own life.
deeperWalk: Hold two people from Mark 10 side by side. The rich young ruler has kept the commandments since childhood, and Mark says "Jesus looked at him and loved him" — yet he walks away sad, because his hands were too full to take Jesus' hand. Then, at the end of the chapter, blind Bartimaeus, who has nothing, throws off his cloak (probably his only possession) and runs to Jesus. One man clutches and grieves; one man drops everything and sees. That's the whole chapter's argument: the kingdom belongs to the empty-handed. And notice how it opens — the daughters of Zelophehad receive an inheritance they could never have claimed under the old rules. Grace keeps moving the last to the front.
bibleThread: "The Son of Man came to give his life as a ransom for many." This is one of the clearest statements in the Gospels of why Jesus came. Every servant act in Mark 10 flows from a King who would kneel lowest of all — to the cross. The rich man couldn't buy eternal life because it was never for sale; it's an inheritance, purchased by Another and received by the empty-handed. Greatness in God's kingdom looks like Jesus, and Jesus looks like a towel, a basin, and a ransom paid for you.
heartCheck: Where am I reaching up for a higher seat instead of down into a servant's joy? | What are my hands too full of to fully take Jesus' hand? | Who is the "Bartimaeus" I'd rather climb past than stop for?
journalPrompt: Jesus, show me the one thing my fingers are wrapped around — and give me the willingness to open my hand. What would it look like to receive from You instead of clutching?
wellnessPractice: Before the day's climbing begins, sit for a moment with open palms. Breathe slowly and pray, "With man it's impossible, but with You it's possible — open my hand." Let the striving loosen.
devPrayer: Jesus, You didn't come to be served but to serve, and You paid a ransom I could never afford. Cure me of the climb. Give me the low, free joy of serving people who can't repay me — and let me find You there at the bottom, towel in hand. Amen.
walkItOut: Do one hidden act of service today for someone who can't repay you and won't even know to thank you. Handle the thankless task, give the credit away, or serve the person it's easiest to overlook.
saveLine: In His kingdom, the way up is down.

dayLabel: Thursday · July 9
editorNote: Before you bring God your longest list of requests, He may want to clear one thing out of the room first. Today's reading names it.
devHeading: Forgiveness clears the room for prayer
devRef: Numbers 31–33 · Mark 11
devIntro: Numbers pauses to list the whole journey, campsite by campsite — God keeps a record of every mile. In Mark 11, Jesus rides in to shouts of Hosanna, clears the temple of everything that had cheapened it, and then ties powerful prayer to a surprising condition: a forgiving heart.
devVerseText: "And when you stand praying, if you hold anything against anyone, forgive them, so that your Father in heaven may forgive you your sins."
devVerseRef: Mark 11:25
devBody: The order in Mark 11 is not an accident. First Jesus clears the temple — drives out what had turned a house of prayer into a marketplace — and then He talks about the kind of faith that moves mountains. And when He names what clogs our prayers, He doesn't say doubt first. He says unforgiveness. A heart full of grudges is a temple full of overturned tables; you can pray in a room like that, but not with much power, because part of you is still gripping a case against someone. Forgiveness isn't pretending the wound didn't matter. It's refusing to carry the debt into God's presence, handing both the person and the pain to the Judge who actually handles those.
devKeyWord: Hosanna — literally "save us now!" The crowd shouts it as praise, half-aware they're begging the King for the very rescue He's riding in to give.
deeperWalk: Don't rush past the cursed fig tree wrapped around the temple scene. Jesus finds a tree full of leaves but no fruit and curses it — then walks straight into a temple full of religious activity but no real prayer. The fig tree is a living parable of the temple: all show, no fruit. Jesus isn't having a bad morning; He's performing a diagnosis. Israel's worship had become a marketplace, loud and busy and empty. And His remedy is startling — He wants a house of prayer marked by faith and forgiveness, not commerce and grudges. The question He's really asking is whether your worship has leaves and no figs: activity without the fruit of a forgiving, trusting heart.
bibleThread: Jesus clears the temple because He is the temple — the true meeting place between God and people. Within days, the veil in that temple will tear, and the whole system of tables and sacrifices will be fulfilled and finished in Him. When He says your Father will forgive you as you forgive others, He's not making forgiveness a transaction you earn; He's describing a heart that has actually received grace. The cross is what makes both possible: God's full forgiveness of you, and your freedom to release everyone else.
heartCheck: What grudge have I been carrying into God's presence? | Is my worship "leaves without figs" — busy activity without a trusting, forgiving heart? | Who do I need to hand to the Judge so my own hands are free?
journalPrompt: Lord, name the person I'm holding something against, and help me release them here on the page. What would change in my prayers if I stopped carrying this into the room?
wellnessPractice: Picture the grudge as a weight in your hands. Take three slow breaths, and on the last exhale open your hands and pray, "I release them to You; I'm not carrying this into Your presence anymore."
devPrayer: Lord, You care enough to clear what doesn't belong in me. Turn over the tables of resentment I've let pile up, make me a house of prayer again, and give me the faith to trust You with the mountain I can't move. I forgive — help me mean it all the way down. Amen.
walkItOut: Before you ask God for anything today, let Him clear one table: name one person you're holding something against and forgive them out loud, on purpose. Then bring Him the mountain you've been asking Him to move.
saveLine: Clear the temple first. Then watch the mountain.

weekFocus: Following the God who leads — even through the wilderness.
dayLabel: Friday · July 10
editorNote: We close the week where the whole Bible closes its argument: on love that gives from the heart, not the leftovers. Let it land before the weekend.
devHeading: Two small coins and a whole heart
devRef: Numbers 34–36, Deuteronomy 1 · Mark 12
devIntro: Numbers finishes drawing the map — borders, and cities of refuge where someone who caused harm could flee and be safe, mercy built into the geography. In Mark 12, Jesus names love as the greatest command, then sits down across from the offering box and shows us what that love actually looks like.
devVerseText: "Truly I tell you, this poor widow has put more into the treasury than all the others. They all gave out of their wealth; but she, out of her poverty, put in everything—all she had to live on."
devVerseRef: Mark 12:43–44
devBody: Earlier in Mark 12 Jesus boils the whole Bible down to two commands — love God with everything, love your neighbor as yourself. Then, instead of ending on theory, He walks to the treasury and sits down to watch people give. The rich drop in large, impressive amounts. A poor widow drops in two coins worth almost nothing. And Jesus says she out-gave every one of them — because they gave from their surplus, and she gave from her heart, "all she had to live on." Loving God isn't about the size of what's in your hands; it's about whether your heart is actually in what you offer. God isn't impressed by leftovers dressed up as generosity. He's moved by the person who gives Him the real thing.
devKeyWord: City of refuge — a town set aside where someone could flee and be safe from vengeance while justice was sorted out. A picture of the God who builds mercy into the map for people who mess up.
deeperWalk: Watch how Mark 12 is arranged. Jesus fields trap after trap — taxes, resurrection, the greatest commandment — silencing every challenger, until "no one dared ask him any more questions." Then, having won every argument, He doesn't hold a press conference. He sits down and notices a widow no one else saw. That's the heart of God in one camera move: away from the powerful and impressive, toward the small and overlooked and wholehearted. And it rhymes with Numbers, where God carves cities of refuge into the land — real places where a person who fell short could run and be safe. The God who counts a widow's two coins is the same God who builds mercy into the map.
bibleThread: The widow gives all she has to live on — and days later, Jesus will do the same, giving up His life "as a ransom for many." She is a living picture of Him. And the cities of refuge in Numbers point the same direction: a place to flee and be safe from the penalty you deserve. The New Testament will say we "have fled to take hold of the hope set before us" — Jesus Himself is our refuge, the city we run to, safe from condemnation. Love God with everything, love your neighbor as yourself — He did both, all the way to a cross, so that you could.
heartCheck: Am I giving God my leftovers dressed up as generosity, or my two small coins — my honest, whole heart? | Who is the overlooked person God is asking me to actually see today? | Where do I need to run to Jesus as my refuge instead of hiding or performing?
journalPrompt: Father, what are the "two small coins" You're asking of me — the ordinary, honest offering I keep holding back for something more impressive? I'll lay them down here.
wellnessPractice: Before the day fills up, give God the first ninety seconds. Breathe slowly and pray, "Here's my whole small heart — not my leftovers." Then step into the day from that offering.
devPrayer: Father, boil me back down to this: love You with everything, love the person in front of me like myself. I don't have much, but You never asked for much — only my whole heart. Take my two small coins, my ordinary day, and let it be given in love, not leftovers. Thank You for building refuge into the map for when I fall short. Amen.
walkItOut: Pick one person and love them like they're you — with your time, your patience, or your two small coins. Give the person who's easy to overlook the version of you that you'd want on your hardest day.
saveLine: The whole walk, in one word: love — all the way in.
tomorrowThread: Tomorrow we open Deuteronomy — Moses looking back over the whole journey, reminding a new generation not to forget the God who carried them.
closingLine: You're not just reading more — you're being discipled, and by making this possible you're helping someone else find Jesus. Thank you for being a Founding Member.
