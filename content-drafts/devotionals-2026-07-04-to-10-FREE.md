## Saturday · July 4 — study day 185 · Numbers 14–16 · Mark 7

- **weekFocus:** This week: following the God who leads — even through the wilderness.
- **dayLabel:** Saturday · July 4
- **readingHeading:** The report you believe decides the life you get
- **readingRef:** Numbers 14–16 · Mark 7
- **readingIntro:** Israel is one step from the promised land. Twelve scouts go in; ten come back shaking — *the people are giants, we felt like grasshoppers.* Two of them, Caleb and Joshua, saw the same land and said: it's good, and God is bigger. The crowd believes the scary report over the faithful God — and it costs them forty years of wandering. Meanwhile, over in Mark 7, Jesus quietly moves the whole conversation from clean hands to clean hearts.
- **verseText:** "Do not be afraid of the people of the land … The Lord is with us. Do not be afraid of them."
- **verseRef:** Numbers 14:9
- **readingAfter:** Here's the quietly terrifying part: the giants were real — but so was God. The difference between the generation that walked in and the one that wandered wasn't the size of the problem. It was which voice they trusted. Fear always sounds like realism; it lists every reason the good thing won't work. Faith doesn't pretend the giants aren't there — it just refuses to make them bigger than God. And Jesus adds the deeper cut in Mark 7: what actually wrecks a person isn't out there — it's the fear, pride, and unbelief we let live in *here*. Guard the heart in the morning, and you've guarded the whole day.
- **keyWord:** Defile — to stain or spoil. Jesus moves it from a hands-and-food issue to a heart issue: what really marks you is what comes *out* of you, not what happens *to* you.
- **makeItRealHeading:** So what — for today?
- **makeItRealBody:** You'll get a report today — from your circumstances, your inbox, your own head — and it'll sound exactly like the truth. Before you believe it, ask whose voice it is. Fear's report ends in retreat; God's ends in *the Lord is with us.* Try this today: when the scary story starts running, say it out loud — "The giants are real, but God is bigger." For example, before the conversation or the task you're quietly dreading, name the fear once, then name one true thing God has promised right beside it — and walk in on the second sentence, not the first.
- **question:** 👉 Where is fear talking you out of a good thing God is actually holding out to you?
- **prayer:** Father, I keep believing the scary report over Your faithful promise. Quiet the voice of fear this morning. Clean my heart from the inside out, and remind me: You are with me. I'm not walking in unarmed. Amen.
- **closingLine:** The giants don't get the last word. God does.
- **communityText:** Someone reading this is standing at their own border, scared to step. Pray them across.

## Sunday · July 5 — study day 186 · Numbers 17–20 · Mark 8

- **weekFocus:** This week: following the God who leads — even through the wilderness.
- **dayLabel:** Sunday · July 5
- **readingHeading:** Who you decide Jesus is changes everything else
- **readingRef:** Numbers 17–20 · Mark 8
- **readingIntro:** God settles a leadership fight by making Aaron's dead staff bud overnight — life out of a dead stick. Then come some of the hardest miles of the wilderness: no water, Moses losing his temper at the rock, Miriam and Aaron dying. And in Mark 8, after feeding thousands, Jesus turns to His friends and asks the one question the whole story hangs on: *Who do you say I am?*
- **verseText:** "'But what about you?' he asked. 'Who do you say I am?' Peter answered, 'You are the Messiah.'"
- **verseRef:** Mark 8:29
- **readingAfter:** Notice Jesus doesn't ask what the crowds think — He's already heard the polls. He wants Peter's own answer, out loud. Because it's one thing to know *about* Jesus and another to say *You are the One* and stake your Monday on it. That confession is the hinge everything else swings on: if He's just a good teacher, you'll take His advice when it's convenient. If He's the Christ, you follow Him into the parts that don't make sense yet — the way Israel had to keep following God past the dry places and the fresh graves. Who He is decides how you'll walk today.
- **keyWord:** Messiah / Christ — God's promised, anointed Rescuer. Calling Jesus "the Christ" means He is the One the whole Old Testament was leaning toward.
- **makeItRealHeading:** So what — for today?
- **makeItRealBody:** Your honest answer to "Who is Jesus?" is already showing up in your calendar and your worries — we trust who we believe He is. Try this today: say it plainly, just once, out loud — "Jesus, You are the Christ" — and then let it decide one thing. For example, the situation you're most anxious about right now: hand it to Him *as the Rescuer*, not as a nice idea. If He's really who Peter said, He can carry it.
- **question:** 👉 If Jesus turned to you this morning and asked, "Who do you say I am?" — what's your honest answer, and does your day match it?
- **prayer:** Lord, like Peter I want to say it and mean it: You are the Christ. Lead me even down the roads I don't understand, past the dry places and the losses. I'm trusting who You are more than what I can see. Amen.
- **closingLine:** He's not asking for a perfect answer. He's asking for an honest one.
- **communityText:** Say one true thing about who Jesus is over someone else this morning.

## Monday · July 6 — study day 187 · Numbers 21–23 · Mark 9

- **weekFocus:** This week: following the God who leads — even through the wilderness.
- **dayLabel:** Monday · July 6
- **readingHeading:** You don't need perfect faith — just look up
- **readingRef:** Numbers 21–23 · Mark 9
- **readingIntro:** Out in the desert, people are dying from snakebites, and God's rescue makes no sense at first. He doesn't clear out the snakes. He tells Moses to put a bronze snake on a pole and says: *look at it, and live.* Don't fight it, don't fix it, don't earn it — just look up. Over in Mark 9, Jesus pulls back the curtain for one blinding moment, then walks straight down into a mess: a desperate dad, a suffering boy, and a prayer so honest it almost hurts.
- **verseText:** "Immediately the boy's father exclaimed, 'I do believe; help me overcome my unbelief!'"
- **verseRef:** Mark 9:24
- **readingAfter:** The bronze serpent is one of the strangest, most tender rescues in the Bible: the cure for the venom isn't a harder fight — it's a look. Lift your eyes, in trust, to what God provided, and you live. (Jesus later said He'd be lifted up the very same way, so anyone who looks to Him is healed for good.) And at the bottom of the mountain, that father chokes out the truest words most of us ever pray — *I believe; help my unbelief.* His faith is half-built and shaking. Jesus doesn't scold him or send him off to come back with more. He heals the boy right there, in the middle of the honest, mixed-up faith.
- **keyWord:** Bronze serpent — the snake on the pole that healed anyone who simply looked. Jesus said He'd be lifted up the same way on the cross, so that whoever looks to Him in trust would live.
- **makeItRealHeading:** So what — for today?
- **makeItRealBody:** You do not need perfect faith this morning. You just need to look up with the little you've got. Try this today: pick the one shaky, half-trusting corner of your life and pray the father's exact prayer over it — "I believe; help my unbelief." For example, the worry you keep white-knuckling — instead of forcing yourself to *feel* more certain, just turn your eyes toward Jesus with the faith you actually have, and leave it lifted up in His hands for the day.
- **question:** 👉 Where do you most need to pray, right now, "I believe — help my unbelief"?
- **prayer:** Jesus, I believe; help my unbelief. Thank You that You don't wait for me to have it all figured out before You come close. I'm looking up with the faith I've got — meet me here. Amen.
- **closingLine:** Healing came by looking, not by fighting. Look up.
- **communityText:** Somebody's faith is shaking this morning. Hold the rope for them.

## Tuesday · July 7 — study day 188 · Numbers 24–26

- **weekFocus:** This week: following the God who leads — even through the wilderness.
- **dayLabel:** Tuesday · July 7
- **readingHeading:** Hired to curse you — and he couldn't
- **readingRef:** Numbers 24–26
- **readingIntro:** A king pays a prophet named Balaam to stand on a hill and curse Israel. Balaam opens his mouth to curse — and blessing comes out. Again. And again. He literally cannot do it, because you cannot curse what God has blessed. And then, out of that hired mouth, comes a promise that reaches all the way to Jesus: a star will rise out of Jacob.
- **verseText:** "A star will come out of Jacob; a scepter will rise out of Israel."
- **verseRef:** Numbers 24:17
- **readingAfter:** Sit with how good this is. There's a whole scheme running against God's people up on that ridge — money changing hands, a prophet-for-hire, three attempts to speak ruin over them — and God turns every curse into a blessing before it lands. Israel doesn't even know it's happening; they're just camped in the valley, living their day. That's the God you follow. Not every plan against you gets a headline; some of the biggest rescues happen on a hill you'll never see. Your job isn't to out-argue every threat — it's to stay under the blessing of the God who already has the last word. (Chapter 25 is the sober flip side: what the curse couldn't do from outside, quiet compromise nearly did from inside. Guard the heart.)
- **keyWord:** Oracle — a message spoken on God's behalf. The irony of Numbers 24: even a prophet paid to harm God's people can only say what God gives him to say.
- **makeItRealHeading:** So what — for today?
- **makeItRealBody:** You will not always see the hill where the curse gets turned into a blessing — but you can trust the God standing on it. Try this today: instead of spending your energy bracing against everything that could go wrong, name one worry you've been treating like a curse hanging over you, and hand it up. For example, that situation you're sure is working against you — pray, "God, You turn curses into blessings; do that here, even if I never see how," and then go live your day in the valley, unafraid.
- **question:** 👉 What threat have you been bracing against that you could instead simply hand to the God who turns curses into blessings?
- **prayer:** Father, thank You that no plan against me gets the final say — You do. Where I've been living braced and afraid, remind me I'm standing under Your blessing, not someone else's curse. Guard my heart from the inside, too. Amen.
- **closingLine:** You can't be cursed out of what God has blessed you into.
- **communityText:** Speak a blessing over someone today instead of a worry.

## Wednesday · July 8 — study day 189 · Numbers 27–30 · Mark 10  *(Pastor's Take day)*

- **weekFocus:** This week: following the God who leads — even through the wilderness.
- **dayLabel:** Wednesday · July 8
- **readingHeading:** The one thing you can't hold and follow at the same time
- **readingRef:** Numbers 27–30 · Mark 10
- **readingIntro:** In Numbers, five sisters step forward to ask for an inheritance the rules never made room for — and God says *they're right*, give it to them. Joshua is handed the baton to lead. And in Mark 10, a good, rich, sincere man runs up to Jesus asking how to inherit eternal life — and walks away sad, because the one thing Jesus asked for was the one thing he couldn't let go of.
- **verseText:** "Jesus looked at them and said, 'With man this is impossible, but not with God; all things are possible with God.'"
- **verseRef:** Mark 10:27
- **readingAfter:** The rich man isn't a villain — he's kept the commandments since he was a kid, and Mark says *Jesus looked at him and loved him.* That's what makes it ache: his hands were just too full to take Jesus' hand. It was never about the money itself; it was about what owned him. And then Jesus says the line that turns the whole thing from guilt into hope — *with God, all things are possible.* The thing you can't seem to release, the grip you can't loosen on your own — that's exactly the impossible God specializes in. You don't white-knuckle your way free. You ask the God of the possible to open your fingers.
- **keyWord:** Inherit — to receive what you could never earn. Both the daughters of Zelophehad and the rich man are asking about inheritance; the gospel answer is that eternal life is *received* from a good Father, not purchased.
- **makeItRealHeading:** So what — for today?
- **makeItRealBody:** There's usually one thing our hands are too full of to fully grab hold of Jesus — a comfort, a plan, a resentment, a need to control. Try this today: ask God to show you the one thing your fingers are wrapped around, and pray for the willingness to open your hand. For example, don't try to force yourself to care less — just say, "God, I can't let go of this on my own; with You it's possible — help me loosen my grip," and watch for the small chance He gives you today to actually do it.
- **question:** 👉 What's the one thing your hands are too full of to fully take Jesus' hand this morning?
- **prayer:** Jesus, You look at me and love me — and You still ask for the thing I'm gripping hardest. I can't pry my own fingers loose. But with You it's possible, so open my hand. I'd rather have You than anything I'm holding. Amen.
- **closingLine:** He loved the man too much to let him settle for full hands and an empty heart.
- **pastorTake:** We hear "sell everything" and flinch — but don't miss what happened first: *Jesus looked at him and loved him.* The hard word came wrapped in affection, not condemnation. That's how God's correction always comes to His kids — never to shame you, always to free you. So when you feel Him putting His finger on the one thing you're clutching, don't hear a landlord demanding rent. Hear a Father who loves you too much to watch you trade Him for something smaller. The rich man walked away sad because he said no to Love, not because he said no to a rule. You get the same invitation this morning — and the same possible-with-God grace to say yes.

## Thursday · July 9 — study day 190 · Numbers 31–33 · Mark 11

- **weekFocus:** This week: following the God who leads — even through the wilderness.
- **dayLabel:** Thursday · July 9
- **readingHeading:** Clear the temple, then trust the mountain to move
- **readingRef:** Numbers 31–33 · Mark 11
- **readingIntro:** Numbers pauses to list the whole journey — stage by stage, campsite by campsite, all the way from Egypt to the edge of home. God keeps a record of every mile you walked with Him. And in Mark 11, Jesus rides into Jerusalem to shouts of *Hosanna,* then does two startling things: He clears the temple of everything that had turned worship into a marketplace, and He tells His friends that real prayer, offered from a forgiving heart, can move mountains.
- **verseText:** "Therefore I tell you, whatever you ask for in prayer, believe that you have received it, and it will be yours."
- **verseRef:** Mark 11:24
- **readingAfter:** Don't rush past the order Jesus puts these in. First He *cleared the temple* — drove out what didn't belong in a house of prayer — and *then* He talked about mountain-moving faith. That's not an accident. A cluttered, unforgiving heart is a temple full of tables; you can't pray from it with any power. And notice what Jesus names as the clutter: not just the money-changers, but *unforgiveness* — "when you stand praying, if you hold anything against anyone, forgive them." The mountains in your life may not move because the temple's too crowded. Clear the room. Then pray like it matters, because it does.
- **keyWord:** Hosanna — literally "save us now!" The crowd shouts it as praise, half-aware they're asking the King for the very rescue He's riding in to give.
- **makeItRealHeading:** So what — for today?
- **makeItRealBody:** Powerful prayer usually starts with a cleared-out heart, not a longer list. Try this today: before you ask God for anything, let Him clear one table — name one person you're holding something against, and forgive them, out loud, on purpose. For example, that grudge you've quietly filed away — say, "God, I let it go; I'm not carrying it into Your presence anymore" — and *then* bring Him the mountain you've been asking Him to move. A clean temple prays with a different kind of confidence.
- **question:** 👉 What table needs clearing out of your heart before you pray your biggest prayer today?
- **prayer:** Lord, You care enough to clear what doesn't belong in me. Turn over the tables of resentment and clutter, make me a house of prayer again — and give me the faith to trust You with the mountain I can't move myself. Amen.
- **closingLine:** Clear the temple first. Then watch the mountain.
- **communityText:** Forgiveness is heavy to lift alone. Ask the community to pray you through one.

## Friday · July 10 — study day 191 · Numbers 34–36, Deut 1 · Mark 12

- **weekFocus:** This week: following the God who leads — even through the wilderness.
- **dayLabel:** Friday · July 10
- **readingHeading:** The whole thing, boiled down to love
- **readingRef:** Numbers 34–36, Deuteronomy 1 · Mark 12
- **readingIntro:** Numbers wraps up drawing the map — borders, cities of refuge where someone who caused harm could run and be safe from revenge, mercy built right into the geography. Deuteronomy 1 opens with Moses looking back over the whole journey. And in Mark 12, someone asks Jesus to name the single most important command in all of Scripture, and He boils the entire Bible down to one word: love.
- **verseText:** "Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind and with all your strength … Love your neighbor as yourself. There is no commandment greater than these."
- **verseRef:** Mark 12:30–31
- **readingAfter:** Everything you've read all week — the wilderness, the leaders, the census lists, the cities of refuge — it all funnels down to this. Jesus takes the whole towering stack of commands and says the point was always two things: love God with everything, and love the person next to you like they're you. Not feelings on a good day — direction. And watch where the chapter ends: a poor widow drops two tiny coins in the offering, and Jesus says she gave more than all the rich people, because she gave from love, not leftovers. That's the whole walk. Not impressive. Not for show. Just heart, all the way in. God has even built refuge into the map for the ones who fall short — so you can love boldly without pretending you're perfect.
- **keyWord:** City of refuge — a town set aside where someone could flee and be safe from vengeance while justice was sorted out. A picture of the God who builds mercy into the map for people who mess up.
- **makeItRealHeading:** So what — for today?
- **makeItRealBody:** If the whole Bible comes down to loving God and loving the person in front of you, then the most spiritual thing you do today might be small and unglamorous. Try this today: pick one person and love them like they're you — with your time, your patience, or your two small coins. For example, the family member or coworker who's easy to be short with — give them the version of you that you'd want on your hardest day, and offer God not your leftovers but your first, honest attention this morning. That's the whole thing.
- **question:** 👉 Who is the one person God is putting in front of you to love like they're you today?
- **prayer:** Father, boil me back down to this: love You with everything, love the person in front of me like myself. Take my two small coins — my ordinary day, my honest heart — and let it be given in love, not leftovers. Thank You for building refuge into the map for when I fall short. Amen.
- **closingLine:** The whole walk, in one word: love — all the way in.
- **communityText:** Name one person you'll love like they're you today, and let someone cheer you on.
